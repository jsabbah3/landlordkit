Charge a late fee above your state's legal cap and, in most states, the entire
fee becomes unenforceable — even though the tenant signed the lease. Some
states void the clause; some add penalties; and an unlawful fee can complicate
an eviction for nonpayment. Yet the caps are scattered across dozens of
statutes, so most lease templates just guess.

Here's the 2026 picture, from the statute-cited data behind LandlordKit's free
late-fee calculator.

## Three regimes

1. **Hard caps (16 states).** A fixed percentage, dollar amount, or formula —
   from Maine's 4% (with a country-strictest 15-day grace period) to New
   Mexico's 10%. New York caps at the *lesser* of $50 or 5%; North Carolina and
   Colorado use the *greater* of a small floor or 5%.
2. **"Reasonable" states (most of the rest).** No fixed number, but the fee
   must approximate actual damages and appear in the lease. Courts have voided
   double-digit percentages as penalties.
3. **Grace-period states.** Separate from caps: several states bar any fee
   until rent is N days late — Massachusetts a full 30 days, Connecticut 9,
   Colorado 7, NY/NC/TN/Delaware 5, Oregon 4, Nevada 3. Maryland's 2025 law
   added county-level grace periods.

## The mistakes that cost landlords

- **Copying a national lease template** with a flat $75–$100 fee into a capped
  state (illegal in NY for rents under $1,000+, over-cap in ME at most rents).
- **Charging on day one** in a grace-period state.
- **Calling the fee "additional rent"** — several states (CO explicitly)
  prohibit reclassifying fees as rent to fast-track eviction.
