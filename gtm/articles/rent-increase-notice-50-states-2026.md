Raising rent legally is mostly about **notice** — and the required notice
varies more than most landlords expect: from 30 days in the majority of states
to 90 days in Oregon and Washington-sized caps on the increase itself.

This table is generated from the statute-cited data behind LandlordKit's free
rent-increase notice generator.

## The patterns

- **30 days is the baseline** for month-to-month tenancies in most states.
- **Tiered notice** is the trap: California requires 90 days when the increase
  exceeds 10% in 12 months; New York scales with tenancy length (30/60/90 days
  at 1 and 2 years); Rhode Island extends to 60 days for tenants 62+.
- **Statewide rent caps** now exist in California (5% + CPI, max 10%), Oregon
  (7% + CPI, max 10%, no increase in year one), and Washington (2025 law,
  ~7% + CPI) — plus local control in NYC, DC, New Jersey's 100+ municipalities,
  and several Maryland counties.
- **No mid-lease increases** anywhere, unless the lease itself allows them.

## Doing it right

Written notice, current and new rent stated, lawful effective date, delivered
provably. The free [rent increase notice generator](https://getlandlordkit.com/tools/rent-increase-notice-generator)
applies your state's period (including the tiers), flags rent-control caveats,
and produces a clean PDF letter.

*General information, not legal advice. Local ordinances can override state
defaults — check your city.*
