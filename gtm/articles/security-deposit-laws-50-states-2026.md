Security deposits are the most regulated — and most litigated — part of a small
landlord's job. Three rules vary dramatically by state: **how fast you must
return the deposit**, **whether you owe the tenant interest while you hold it**,
and **what happens if you get it wrong** (in many states: two to three times the
deposit, plus the tenant's attorney fees).

We maintain these rules as structured, statute-cited data for LandlordKit's
free calculators. Here's the complete 2026 picture.

## Return deadlines: 14 to 60 days

Every state sets a deadline to return the deposit after move-out. The range is
wide: Alaska, Arizona, and Hawaii give you 14 days, while Alabama and Arkansas
allow 60. Most states cluster at 30. Nearly all require an **itemized
statement** for any deductions — and several toughen the deadline or the
penalty when deductions are involved.

## Interest: only ~15 states require it

A persistent myth says all landlords owe deposit interest. In fact only about
15 states (including DC) — plus cities like Chicago — require it, and the
mechanics differ everywhere: Massachusetts is a flat 5% after one year; Ohio
pays 5% only on the amount above one month's rent; Connecticut and Maryland
peg the rate to an annually published index; New York applies only to
buildings with six or more units.

## The penalty patterns worth knowing

- **Double/treble damages** for missed deadlines or bad-faith retention:
  Colorado (treble, willful), Maryland and Massachusetts (up to 3x), many
  others at 2x.
- **Forfeiture:** in several states, missing the deadline forfeits your right
  to keep *any* of the deposit — even for legitimate damage.
- **Burden of proof:** itemization plus receipts/photos is what wins disputes.
